Unprecedented access to small business loan data

It seems that no section of the country was untouched by enterpreneurs seeking relief from the effects of the government shutdown in reaction to the Coronavirus. The release of lending records from the US Treasury in conjunction with the Small Business Association, albeit under unfortunate circumstances, allows an unusual glimpse into small businesses affected by the crisis.

Initial analysis

The purpose of the loans was to help businesses save jobs. The potential expenditure per capita of funds for the larger loan amounts came in at $2,000 and the under $150k disbursements at $8,000 per job saved. This assessment is based on calculations infered from statements Steve Mnuchin made, namely that the sum of the loans over $150k account for 75% of the total lent and was borrowed by only 13% of enterprises.

Machine learning also suggested a dependency between number of jobs "saved" and loan ammounts as it gave this feature the most weight.

Even though the largest of loans went to seemingly the largest employers, the question arises as to how effective $2000 per job would be in helping employees in comparison to the smaller business loans which had a higher amount of money per person ratio.

Businesses loans and the jobs saved.

The pie charts below reflect the loan volume in each general job category along with the jobs saved. This helps to evaluate where the money went and which industries supported retention of the greatest number of jobs. For example, the visualizations suggest that the "Acommodation and Food Services" category saved the greatest percentage of employees in proportion to how much was borrowed and "Construction" looks to be the reverse.

The Banks

Bank of America and JP Morgan Chase were at the top of the list of lenders by count of loans, but financial tech organizations such as Kabbage and Celtic Bank made a strong showing.

The next step

There is a lot here, and the number of lines of inquiry seem potentially endless. A few ideas are to obtain granularity on loan dispersement per job saved in various categories, regional lending differences such as rural vs urban, differences between banks in various states.

One of many candidates for deeper analysis is how the loan information provides insight into various ethnic groups. For example, Asians borrowed almost twice as much as Hispanics while only being one third the size of the Hispanic population. There could be any number of reasons for this but examining rates of self employment, business types and/or cultural attitudes towards business investment may be illuminating. Additionally, it is possible that Asians answered the racial inquiry more often than Hispanics which would skew the results.

A personal note

Small business is often touted as the "back bone" of America, and yet it seems that a mandate in reaction to a virus "cut business owners off at the knees". Even though this was mostly a pipeline project for me, compassion for the extreme nature of what small business people are experiencing led me to look at not only the general trends in an attempt to understand the effects Covid is having on our economy, I found myself being incessantly intrigued by what the data suggests about individual establishments and outliers. Some of my favorite restaurants, professional organizations, colleagues and friends appear in this database, and I am grateful for the support that this program has afforded everyone. From the perspective of investigating data outliers, there were also unexpected findings such as that a "spa" for pets in Southern California received a loan north of $1 million while most of the "people spas" received loans well under 50k. I found a business (pictured below) that received potentially $3 million from the PPP with one review on Google in a foreign language, and a residential address.
